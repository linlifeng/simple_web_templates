Foodiee

Foodiee is a home cooking assistant app that combines the functions of grocery shopping and recipe searching. It not only helps the users deliver their ingredients but also helps them plan and manage their ingredients better, and inspires the users to try new ideas.

  • Timeline: 12 weeks
  • Role: Researcher & UX/UI Designer
  • Tools: Figma & Miro

Problem

After Covid-19 broke out, more people were forced to stay home and cook more. Although everybody knew, cooking at home is cheaper and healthier, not everybody enjoys it, and worse, they started losing passion for it. Why?

Primary Research

After the secondary research, I decided to dig deeper into the primary research. I chose the user interview as my research method because when the topic is cooking, nothing comes close to the personal experience a user could provide.

Survey

Before the interview, I conducted a survey among potential users. It included 8 questions and 15 participants. The questionnaire helped me choose the right interviewees and gain quantitative data.

User Interview

After carefully picking up the interviewees, I conducted 6 interviews and collected a large amount of information. Not only did I learn the entirety of their cooking experiences, but I also grasp their habits and emotions while planning and making meals.

Synthesizing

After the interview, all qualitative data were converted into an affinity map, then coded, sorted, regrouped, and prioritized.

Key Findings

Even though different users’ cooking and shopping habits are varied, with visualized data, I could still see many overlaps in their thinking patterns and behaviors.

  • Interviewees would want to use grocery delivery if the cost isn’t too high.
  • Most of the interviewees do planning in their heads while shopping for groceries.
  • Half of the interviewees get cooking ideas from browsing images/videos.
  • Interviewees think the meal kit idea is good, but costs too much and didn’t like the taste of it.
  • Most of them would use their own recipes or modify the recipes they find somewhere else.

Personas

After analyzing the information, I decided to divide the end users into two groups, one older user group, which has a bigger family structure, also has a longer cooking experience. Another group is the younger users group who just started life on his/her own, and doesn't have much cooking experience, but are forced to cook more to save money.

Define

We know the WHY, HOW can we help?

Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

The JTBD frame helps me to determine users’ motivation and desired outcomes. It acts as a driving principle in determining what functions will help to solve users’ meal-making problems.

How Might We (HMW)

I use the HMW frame to narrow down the JTBD data into focused questions to be answered.

  • How might we help the users to plan their meals better?
  • How might we help the user to find and buy the right things?
  • How might we inspire users with the right recipes?

Testing

Test the prototype and find the hidden problems.

Test Plan

After the prototype is done, I conduct 5 moderated usability tests. The goal of this test is to uncover usability problems in the red route and discover user habits for further improvement.

    Test Subjectives

  • Can the login process onboarding pages help users figure out some important functions of this app?
  • Can users find items and complete the checkout process?
  • How do participants respond to the scanning and pantry functions?
  • Can users walk through the scanning process without any confusion?
  • Can users find matching recipes without any confusion?

Test Results and Iterations

The test results are interesting and very helpful. I was able to find some major issues after 5 tests.FULL REPORT

    Key Findings

  • Login takes too long and needs a better flow to take users to the Home Page.
  • Detail matters: users brought up many wording issues.
  • Complicated functions would scare the users.

Some of the iteration screens

After I fixed all the critical issues I ran 5 more tests. The user flows after iteration were significantly improved. Users could go through all tasks much more smoothly.

Conclusion

What did I learn?

During this project, I encountered many problems, these problems taught me to always remember that I'm designing for the users, not myself. Look at things from more than one perspective, and try to design the minimum viable product instead of a perfect product.

    Takeaways

  • At first, I was going to design a grocery pick-up service. But during research, I realized I didn’t consider those who can't drive or don’t have cars. Design should be more inclusive.
  • When doing the user survey, I didn't think demographic would impact this project, but it does! People with different cultures and family structures have very different shopping and cooking habits.
  • Foodiee's design is a “BIG bite” I kept adding more features and trying to make it "does it all" and "perfect" but didn't realize making it right is more important.
  • Testing script is important, but the testing flow is important as well. Sometimes, the right design might seem off when testing it in the wrong order. Figuring out whether the issue is in the design or the test is something I would need to improve in my future projects.
